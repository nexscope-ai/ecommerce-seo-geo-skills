# Ecommerce GEO readiness framework

Use this framework as a qualitative diagnostic aid, not as a claim about proprietary AI ranking factors. Readiness and measured AI visibility are different outcomes.

## Contents

1. Access and search foundation
2. Product facts and identity
3. Structured data and consistency
4. Buyer-intent coverage
5. Trust and proof
6. Answerability and content structure
7. Media and page experience
8. External visibility evidence
9. Optional heuristic scoring

## 1. Access and search foundation

Inspect only with live or technical evidence:

- Page is accessible, indexable, canonicalized appropriately, and linked internally.
- Important text is present in rendered HTML.
- Search controls do not unintentionally block the page.
- Page experience and mobile rendering do not prevent use.

Do not infer these checks from copy alone.

## 2. Product facts and identity

Look for:

- Clear product, brand, category, audience, and use case
- Visible specifications, variants, price, availability, and policies
- Consistent facts across the page and structured data
- Important information available as text, not only images

There is no universal requirement that a brand appear within an exact word count.

## 3. Structured data and consistency

Look for accurate Product and Offer-family markup where the platform permits it. Confirm that values match visible content.

Structured data can help systems understand a page and enable eligible search features. It does not guarantee rankings, rich results, AI citations, or recommendations. Google states that its AI search features require no special AI schema beyond normal search eligibility and accurate structured data.

## 4. Buyer-intent coverage

Assess whether the page answers relevant questions about:

- Fit and audience
- Use cases and limitations
- Specifications and compatibility
- Price or value context
- Alternatives and differentiators
- Shipping, returns, warranty, and support

Use genuine demand, support, review, or query evidence when available.

## 5. Trust and proof

Check for visible, verifiable evidence such as:

- Genuine reviews and ratings
- Clear seller and brand identity
- Accurate shipping, return, warranty, and contact information
- Certifications or tests with traceable sources
- Consistent third-party evidence when supplied

Never create or embellish trust signals.

## 6. Answerability and content structure

Useful patterns include:

- Direct explanations of what the product is and who it serves
- Specific facts in clear sentences and tables
- Descriptive headings
- Concise answers to genuine buyer questions
- Honest limitations and comparison criteria

FAQ formatting can improve usability, but it is not a universal citation mechanism. Google FAQ rich results are generally limited to eligible authoritative government and health sites.

## 7. Media and page experience

Review:

- Relevant crawlable images and video
- Descriptive alt text that serves accessibility
- Responsive delivery and observed performance evidence
- Text alternatives for important information shown in media

Do not use image filenames, WebP, or an arbitrary file-size threshold as a standalone AI-visibility score.

## 8. External visibility evidence

Actual visibility requires dated, query-level evidence such as:

- Whether the product was mentioned
- Recommended position or role
- Citations or source URLs
- Competitors shown for the same query
- Model, market, language, and run time

Do not infer actual mentions from page readiness alone.

## 9. Optional heuristic scoring

If the user explicitly wants a score and enough evidence is available, score each area:

- `0` — weak or blocked with confirmed evidence
- `1` — partial or mixed
- `2` — strong with confirmed evidence
- `Unknown` — not inspected

Report the number of scored and unknown areas. Do not convert unknowns to zero, forecast a future score, or claim a score predicts citations, traffic, or sales.
